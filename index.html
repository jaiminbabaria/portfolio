Featured Project
Flagship Project · 2025

FairFinance AI — Auditing socioeconomic bias in LLM-generated financial advice.

APPLIED ML PROJECT  ·  UNIVERSITY OF ILLINOIS CHICAGO  ·  648 RESPONSES  ·  3 MODELS  ·  1 UNCOMFORTABLE FINDING

Do large language models give worse financial advice to people who sound poor? Three frontier models. A controlled audit. The answer was both yes and methodologically inconvenient.

I designed a controlled audit framework grounding prompts in the Chicago Community Area Hardship Index, then evaluated GPT-4o-mini, LLaMA-3.3 70B, and Gemini-3 Flash across 648 paired responses. To score the responses at concept-level rather than surface-level, I built a hybrid scoring algorithm combining regex pattern matching with DeBERTa-v3 natural language inference — validated against human annotation on 150 labels.

The crucial step was a counterfactual signal ablation isolating which demographic cue actually drives the gap. Stripping signals one at a time across all three models pointed to a single causal driver — not the zip code, not the job title.

  • FINDING 01 Statistically significant accuracy gaps favored high-SES users across every model audited: GPT ΔA = +0.048 · LLaMA ΔA = +0.133 · Gemini ΔA = +0.124. All three failed the 80% Disparate Impact Rule.
  • FINDING 02 Counterfactual ablation isolated education level as the sole causal driver of the accuracy gap across all models (p < 0.01). Neighborhood and job title produced no statistically significant effect.
  • FINDING 03 Regex-only evaluation completely missed Gemini's bias (p = 0.28 regex vs. p < 0.001 NLI) — exposing a methodological gap in standard fairness auditing.
  • FINDING 04 Hybrid NLI scoring achieved Cohen's κ = 0.977 against human annotation — near-perfect inter-rater reliability at scale.
